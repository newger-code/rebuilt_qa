Rehab Estimate — How the Formula Works

Internal reference for the team. Plain English, no code.

The Big Idea

We don't add line items. Instead, each answer shifts the property's overall condition score, which drives a per-sqft multiplier. That multiplier prices the whole rehab as one number.

Why? Because rehab costs compound. A house that needs HVAC and roof and plumbing costs more per-sqft than just adding those three quotes. Systems interact, demo cascades, contractors mobilize once. The multiplier captures that naturally.

Think of it like a credit score for the house. Each answer nudges the score up or down. The score determines what "tier" of rehab you're looking at — and the cost follows.

Step 1 — Condition Score

Every property starts at a base score of 45 (C territory — moderate). Each answer adds or subtracts points. The total score determines the grade.

Step 2 — Score → Multiplier

The score maps to a multiplier on a smooth curve. This isn't a hard grade band — it slides continuously so the estimate never "jumps" between grades.

ScoreMultiplierGrade Zone
1002A — cosmetic
802A — move-in
604B — minor
407C — moderate
2010D — heavy
013F — gut
-6022F⁻ — worst case structural

Between these anchor points, the multiplier interpolates smoothly. Score 50 = multiplier ~5.5, not a hard jump from 7 to 4.

Step 3 — The Final Calculation

Est. Rehab = age_factor × sqft × multiplier + boost

Age factor: Older homes cost more per-sqft to rehab (older materials, code upgrades, surprises). A 1970 home ≈ 2.3x, a 1990 home ≈ 1.8x, a 2010 home ≈ 0.8x.

Sqft: Minimum 1,200 sqft floor (smaller homes still have base system costs).

Boost: Newer homes (<40 yrs) in bad condition get a bonus — systems are fresher but cosmetically wrecked, so there's a floor on rehab cost even for "young" homes.

Worked Example

Property: 1970 build, 1,400 sqft

1
Start at score 45 (C territory)
2
Seller says: Roof 20+ yrs (-8), Active leaks (-12), Original plumbing (-5), Fuse box (-6), No kitchen remodel (-5)
45 - 8 - 12 - 5 - 6 - 5 = 9 → Grade F
3
Score 9 → multiplier ≈ 11.7 (between anchors 0→13 and 20→10)
4
Age factor for 1970 = 2.33
2.33 × 1,400 × 11.7 = $38,177
5
Now seller also says: Foundation major issues (-15), Original windows (-4)
9 - 15 - 4 = -10 → still Grade F, but deeper Multiplier at -10 ≈ 15.2
2.33 × 1,400 × 15.2 = $49,594 Added ~$11k just from two more answers. The foundation hit (-15) is the biggest single-answer swing in the system.

FAQ for the Team

"I selected HVAC 15+ but the estimate only went up $3k, not $8k for a new unit?"
Correct. The -7 points shifts the overall condition tier. Some of that HVAC cost is already baked into the per-sqft rate at the current grade. You're not adding a line item — you're telling the model the property is in worse overall shape.

"Why does Foundation Major (-15) hit so much harder than anything else?"
Because foundation work is the most expensive single trade, affects everything above it, and signals hidden problems. It's intentionally the heaviest weight in the system.

"The score went negative — is that a bug?"
No. Negative scores represent truly terrible properties where nearly everything is bad. The multiplier keeps climbing into "worst case structural" territory (up to 22). The gauge slider pins at the left edge.

"Why does the same answer change the estimate by different amounts depending on what else I've selected?"
Because the multiplier curve isn't linear. Going from score 60→53 (B→C transition) is a bigger $/sqft jump than going from 30→23 (already deep in D). The worse things already are, the less each additional bad answer moves the needle — diminishing returns, just like real rehab.
